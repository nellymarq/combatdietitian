Introduction

Water is the most abundant molecule in the human body — roughly 60% of body mass in males and 50-55% in females. A 2% loss in body water impairs endurance performance. A 3-4% loss impairs strength, power, and cognitive function. A 5%+ loss is medically dangerous.

For performance nutrition practitioners, hydration science sits at the intersection of daily health, training performance, and — in weight-class sports — deliberate manipulation. This module covers the physiology of fluid balance, practical hydration assessment, electrolyte management, and environmental considerations. The manipulation of hydration for weight cutting is covered in the Combat Sports specialization track — this module provides the physiological foundation that makes those strategies understandable and safe.

Fluid Balance Physiology

Total Body Water Distribution

Total body water (TBW) is distributed across two compartments:

  • Intracellular fluid (ICF): ~65% of TBW. Water inside cells. Critical for cellular function, enzyme activity, and metabolic processes.
  • Extracellular fluid (ECF): ~35% of TBW. Includes plasma (blood volume), interstitial fluid (between cells), and transcellular fluid (CSF, synovial fluid, etc.).

Water moves between compartments via osmosis — driven by solute concentration gradients. Sodium (Na+) is the primary extracellular solute; potassium (K+) is the primary intracellular solute. This is why sodium intake directly affects extracellular water volume — and why sodium manipulation is central to weight-cutting protocols.

Regulation: The Thirst-ADH-Aldosterone Axis

The body regulates fluid balance through three interconnected mechanisms:

Fluid Regulation Mechanisms

Mechanism Trigger Response Practical Implication
ThirstPlasma osmolality rises ~2%Conscious urge to drinkAlready 1-2% dehydrated by the time you feel thirsty. Not reliable for athletes during intense training.
ADH (Vasopressin)Elevated plasma osmolality or reduced blood volumeKidneys reabsorb water (concentrated urine)Dark urine = ADH working to conserve water = you're already underhydrated.
Aldosterone (RAAS)Reduced blood volume, low sodiumKidneys reabsorb sodium (water follows)This is the mechanism exploited in water loading protocols — chronic overhydration suppresses aldosterone, then when water intake drops, the body continues excreting water at a high rate.

Key insight for practitioners: The body's regulatory systems are designed for survival, not performance optimization. Thirst lags behind actual fluid needs during exercise. ADH and aldosterone respond to deficits, not to optimal hydration. This is why planned hydration strategies outperform "drink when thirsty" for athletes in training and competition.

Sweat: The Primary Fluid Loss Pathway During Exercise

Sweat Rate Variability

Sweat rates vary enormously between individuals and contexts:

Moderate training, temperate1.0-1.5Typical gym conditions

A 90kg fighter sparring for 60 minutes in a warm gym could lose 2-3 liters of sweat — that's 2-3% of body mass in a single session. Without planned replacement, they start the next session already dehydrated.

Sweat Composition: It's Not Just Water

Sweat contains electrolytes — primarily sodium, with smaller amounts of potassium, calcium, and magnesium:

Sodium is the electrolyte that matters most during exercise. An athlete with a moderate sweat rate (1.5 L/hr) and average sodium concentration (50 mmol/L) loses approximately 1,725 mg of sodium per hour. Over a 2-hour training session, that's 3,450 mg — more than the entire daily adequate intake (1,500 mg) recommended for sedentary adults.

This is why blanket advice to "reduce sodium intake" is inappropriate for athletes. Athletes in heavy training may need 3,000-7,000+ mg sodium per day depending on sweat losses.

Practical Hydration Assessment

You can't manage what you don't measure. Here are the field-practical methods for assessing hydration status:

1. Body Weight Change (Gold Standard for Acute Assessment)

The simplest and most reliable method: weigh the athlete before and after training in minimal clothing.

Sweat Rate Calculation

Sweat Rate (L/hr) = (Pre-weight kg - Post-weight kg + Fluid consumed L - Urine volume L) / Exercise duration hr

Simplified: if the athlete doesn't drink or urinate during the session, the weight change IS the sweat loss.

Worked example: An 80kg athlete weighs 78.5kg after a 90-minute sparring session. They drank 500mL during the session.

Sweat loss = (80 - 78.5) + 0.5 = 2.0L in 1.5hr = 1.33 L/hr sweat rate

Repeat this across different session types (sparring, conditioning, S&C) and environments (gym, outdoor, hot, temperate) to build an individualized sweat rate profile. This is far more useful than generic "drink 8 glasses a day" recommendations.

2. Urine Color

A practical daily indicator — not precise, but directionally useful:

Urine Color Scale

1-2: Well hydrated
3-4: Adequately hydrated
5-6: Mildly dehydrated — increase fluid intake
7-8: Significantly dehydrated — rehydrate before training

Adapted from Armstrong et al. (1994). Note: certain foods (beets), supplements (B vitamins), and medications can alter urine color independent of hydration.

Best practice: Assess first morning urine (before eating or drinking). This is the most consistent indicator of baseline hydration status. Athletes should aim for a pale yellow (3-4 on the scale) upon waking.

3. Urine Specific Gravity (USG)

A refractometer provides a numerical measure of urine concentration. More precise than color alone.

  • <1.010: Well hydrated
  • 1.010-1.020: Adequate
  • 1.020-1.030: Dehydrated
  • >1.030: Significantly dehydrated

Note for combat sports practitioners: ONE Championship uses urine specific gravity as a hydration test before weigh-ins. Athletes must pass the hydration test (USG <1.021) before they are allowed to weigh in. This is the mechanism by which ONE bans dehydration-based weight cutting — a model that is spreading to other organizations.

Calculating Daily Hydration Targets

Baseline Daily Intake

A practical starting point for daily water intake:

Baseline: 35-40 mL per kg body weight per day

80kg athlete = 2,800-3,200 mL/day baseline (before training losses)

This baseline covers metabolic water needs, respiratory losses, and non-exercise urinary/fecal losses. Training losses are added on top.

Training Day Adjustment

Total daily target = Baseline + Training sweat losses

Using our 80kg athlete with a 1.33 L/hr sweat rate in a 90-minute session:

Baseline: 3,000 mL + Training loss: 2,000 mL = 5,000 mL total for this training day

On rest days, the baseline alone (3,000 mL) is adequate. This is another dimension of periodization — even water intake should scale with training demand.

Rehydration After Training

The goal is to replace 125-150% of fluid lost during the session within 2-4 hours post-training (Sawka et al., 2007). The extra 25-50% accounts for ongoing urine and respiratory losses during the recovery period.

For a 2.0L sweat loss: drink 2.5-3.0L in the 2-4 hours after training.

Why not just chug 3 liters immediately? Rapid consumption of plain water triggers a diuretic response — the kidneys detect blood dilution and increase urine output, flushing much of what you drank. Adding sodium to post-training fluids (or consuming salty foods alongside water) improves retention because sodium maintains plasma osmolality, keeping ADH elevated and reducing the diuretic response.

Electrolyte Management

Sodium: The Performance Electrolyte

Sodium is the most critical electrolyte for athletes because:

  1. It's lost in the largest quantities through sweat
  2. It regulates extracellular fluid volume (blood volume, plasma volume)
  3. It drives fluid absorption in the gut (sodium-glucose cotransport)
  4. It's essential for nerve impulse transmission and muscle contraction

How Much Sodium?

Daily Sodium Targets for Athletes

Scenario mg/day Context
Rest day, temperate2,000-3,000Normal dietary sodium is adequate
Moderate training, temperate3,000-5,000Add salty foods or electrolyte drink
Hard training, temperate4,000-6,000Deliberate sodium supplementation
Any training, hot environment5,000-8,000+Aggressive replacement essential
Salty sweater in heat7,000-10,000+Individual assessment critical

These numbers shock people accustomed to the <2,300 mg/day guideline from the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. That guideline is for sedentary adults at risk of hypertension — not athletes losing liters of sweat daily. Blanket sodium restriction for athletes is unsupported by the evidence and potentially harmful (Mente et al., 2016).

Potassium, Magnesium, and Calcium

While sodium dominates the acute conversation, the other electrolytes matter for ongoing health and performance:

  • Potassium (3,500-4,700 mg/day): Intracellular electrolyte. Critical for muscle contraction and cardiac function. Found in fruits, vegetables, potatoes, dairy, and legumes. Most athletes get enough from a well-structured diet; supplementation is rarely needed.
  • Magnesium (400-420 mg/day for males, 310-320 for females): Involved in 300+ enzymatic reactions including energy production, muscle relaxation, and sleep quality. Athletes in heavy training are at higher risk of deficiency. Food sources: dark leafy greens, nuts, seeds, dark chocolate. Consider supplementation (200-400mg magnesium glycinate or citrate before bed) if dietary intake is insufficient.
  • Calcium (1,000-1,300 mg/day): Bone health, muscle contraction, nerve transmission. Dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, canned fish with bones. Particularly important for female athletes and any athlete with low energy availability (RED-S risk).

Oral Rehydration Science

Not all fluids rehydrate equally. The composition of a rehydration solution affects how quickly and completely the body absorbs it.

The Sodium-Glucose Cotransport Mechanism

The small intestine absorbs water most efficiently when both sodium and glucose are present. The sodium-glucose cotransporter (SGLT1) on intestinal cells pulls sodium and glucose into the cell together — water follows by osmosis. This is the physiological basis for oral rehydration solutions (ORS) and sports drinks.

Practical takeaway: For rapid rehydration (post-training, post-weigh-in), a solution with sodium (500-1,000 mg/L) and a low-moderate carbohydrate concentration (2-6%) is absorbed faster and retained better than plain water. This is the physiological basis for the rehydration protocols used in the Combat Sports specialization track.

Environmental Considerations

Heat

Hot environments increase sweat rate by 50-100% or more. Additional considerations:

  • Pre-training sodium loading: Consuming 10-25 mEq sodium (230-575 mg) with 300-500 mL water 60-90 minutes before exercise in heat increases plasma volume and improves thermoregulation (Sims et al., 2007).
  • Heat acclimatization nutrition: During the first 5-10 days in a hot environment, plasma volume expands and sweat sodium concentration decreases. Ensure adequate sodium intake during this adaptation period.
  • Increase fluid monitoring frequency: Pre/post-training weigh-ins become essential, not optional, in hot conditions.

Cold

Cold environments blunt the thirst mechanism and increase urinary losses (cold-induced diuresis). Athletes in cold environments are often more dehydrated than those in heat because they don't feel thirsty. Planned fluid intake is critical regardless of perceived thirst.

Altitude

Altitude increases respiratory water loss (drier air, increased ventilation rate) and urinary losses (the body reduces plasma volume as part of altitude acclimatization). Fluid needs increase by approximately 500-1,000 mL/day at altitude (>2,500m). Iron status also matters at altitude — covered in Module 5 (Recovery & Sleep) in the context of adaptation support.

Hyponatremia: The Danger of Overhydration

Overhydration is real and dangerous. Exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) occurs when athletes drink so much water that blood sodium concentration drops below 135 mmol/L. Symptoms range from nausea and confusion to seizures, coma, and death.

EAH is most common in:

  • Endurance events lasting 4+ hours (marathons, ultramarathons, Ironman)
  • Slower athletes who drink at every aid station regardless of thirst
  • Athletes who drink only plain water with no sodium

Prevention: Drink to thirst during events lasting 4+ hours (do not force fluid beyond thirst). Include sodium in hydration plans. Avoid pure water during prolonged exercise — use solutions containing sodium.

In combat sports and most team sports, EAH is rare because events are shorter. However, fighters undergoing aggressive rehydration after weigh-in should use sodium-containing solutions, not plain water, for both effectiveness and safety.

Building an Individualized Hydration Plan

A complete hydration plan for an athlete includes:

  1. Baseline daily target: 35-40 mL/kg body weight (e.g., 80kg athlete = 2,800-3,200 mL baseline)
  2. Individualized sweat rate: Measured via pre/post-training weigh-ins across session types and environments
  3. Training day adjustment: Baseline + 125-150% of estimated sweat loss
  4. Sodium strategy: Scale with training intensity and environment (2,000-8,000+ mg/day depending on conditions)
  5. Monitoring: First-morning urine color (aim for pale yellow). Pre/post-training weight in hot environments or hard sessions.
  6. Rehydration fluid: Include sodium (500-1,000 mg/L) in post-training fluids for better retention
  7. Environmental adjustment: Increase all targets by 50-100% in heat. Monitor more carefully in cold (suppressed thirst). Add 500-1,000 mL/day at altitude.

Key Takeaways

  1. Hydration is performance-critical. Even 2% dehydration impairs endurance; 3-4% impairs strength, power, and cognition.
  2. Thirst is a lagging indicator. Athletes need planned hydration strategies, not "drink when thirsty" during training.
  3. Measure sweat rate. Pre/post-training weigh-ins across session types and environments give you individualized data instead of generic guidelines.
  4. Sodium is the key electrolyte. Athletes in heavy training need far more than sedentary guidelines suggest (3,000-8,000+ mg/day). Blanket sodium restriction is inappropriate for this population.
  5. Rehydration fluid composition matters. Sodium + glucose in solution is absorbed faster and retained better than plain water. This is the physiological basis of oral rehydration science.
  6. Environment changes everything. Heat doubles sweat rates. Cold blunts thirst. Altitude increases respiratory and urinary losses. Adjust hydration plans for the context.

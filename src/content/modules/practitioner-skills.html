Introduction

Modules 1-5 covered the science — energy systems, periodization, hydration, supplements, and recovery. This final module covers the practice — how to assess athletes, measure body composition, track progress, maintain adherence, and know when to escalate beyond your scope.

Knowledge without application is academic. This module bridges the gap between knowing what to recommend and actually delivering results with real athletes in real-world conditions.

The Structured Nutrition Assessment

Every athlete relationship starts with an assessment. A structured intake captures the information you need to design an individualized plan — and it surfaces red flags that require immediate attention.

The 7-Domain Intake Framework

Domain What to Capture Red Flags
1. Medical HistoryConditions, medications, allergies, injuries, surgeries, menstrual health (female athletes)Amenorrhea, disordered eating history, frequent stress fractures, GI conditions, diabetes
2. Training ProfileWeekly schedule, session types, volume, intensity, competition calendarExtreme training volume without adequate fueling, overtraining indicators
3. Body CompositionCurrent weight, goal weight, body fat % (if available), weight historyUnrealistic weight goals, rapid weight fluctuations, obsessive weighing behaviors
4. Dietary HistoryCurrent eating pattern, meals/day, cooking ability, food access, restrictions, past dietsExtreme restriction, elimination diets without medical basis, very low calorie intake relative to training
5. Supplement InventoryAll current supplements with doses and timingUncertified products for drug-tested athletes, banned substances, excessive stimulant use
6. LifestyleSleep (duration, quality), work schedule, stress level, alcohol/caffeine habits, travelChronic sleep deficit (<6 hours), high alcohol use, extreme stress without management strategies
7. Goals & ExpectationsPrimary goal, timeline, what they've tried, what they expect from youUnrealistic timelines, expectations that conflict with health (e.g., "I need to lose 30lbs in 2 weeks for a fight")

Screening for Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)

RED-S (formerly the Female Athlete Triad) is a syndrome caused by low energy availability (LEA) — when caloric intake is insufficient relative to exercise energy expenditure. It affects both male and female athletes and causes cascading health and performance consequences.

RED-S Warning Signs to Screen For

  • • Menstrual dysfunction (females): Amenorrhea (absence), oligomenorrhea (irregular), late menarche
  • • Recurrent stress fractures or bone injuries disproportionate to training load
  • • Unexplained performance decline despite adequate training
  • • Frequent illness (immune suppression from LEA)
  • • Mood disturbances: irritability, depression, anxiety disproportionate to life stressors
  • • GI complaints: bloating, constipation (often from inadequate fiber + overall intake)
  • • Bradycardia: resting heart rate below expected for fitness level
  • • Cold intolerance: often a sign of metabolic suppression
  • • Disordered eating behaviors: food restriction, rigid rules, fear of specific foods, binge-restrict cycles

What to do when you identify RED-S risk: This is beyond nutrition scope alone. RED-S requires a multidisciplinary approach — refer to a sports medicine physician for clinical evaluation. Your role is to identify the risk, begin addressing energy availability (increase intake, reduce exercise energy expenditure, or both), and coordinate with the medical team.

The IOC REDs CAT2 (Clinical Assessment Tool, Version 2) is the current gold standard for structured RED-S screening. It involves three steps: initial screening, severity/risk stratification, and physician-led diagnosis. As a nutrition practitioner, you perform Step 1 (screening) and refer for Steps 2-3 when indicated.

Body Composition Assessment

Body composition data informs everything from caloric targets to weight class decisions. But the method you choose determines the accuracy and consistency of that data.

Assessment Methods Compared

Method Accuracy Cost Practical? Best Use
DEXA ScanHigh$75-150/scanLow (requires facility)Gold standard for research and baseline. Provides regional body comp (useful for rehab).
BodPod (ADP)High$40-75/scanLow (requires facility)No radiation. Good reliability. Less common than DEXA.
Bioelectrical Impedance (BIA)Moderate$0-500 (device)HighGood for tracking trends. Affected by hydration — standardize conditions (morning, fasted, same time).
Skinfold CalipersModerate$15-200 (calipers)HighPractitioner-dependent accuracy. Same person should measure each time. Good for tracking changes.
Circumference MeasurementsLow (for BF%)$5 (tape)HighestMilitary body comp standards use this. Not accurate for BF% but useful for tracking trends and compliance.
Scale WeightLow (for comp)$20-50HighestTracks total mass, not composition. Use for daily monitoring, not body comp assessment. 7-day rolling average smooths daily fluctuations.

Practical Recommendations

  • For baselines and key checkpoints: DEXA or BodPod if accessible (start of camp, mid-camp, post-competition). Provides the most accurate reference points.
  • For ongoing monitoring: BIA (InBody, SECA) or skinfolds, standardized to the same conditions every time — same time of day, same hydration state, same assessor (for skinfolds).
  • For daily tracking: Scale weight first thing in the morning, post-void, in underwear. Track the 7-day rolling average, not individual daily readings. Daily weight fluctuates 1-3kg from water, glycogen, and gut contents — the trend matters, not the number.
  • For all methods: consistency of conditions matters more than the method's absolute accuracy. A BIA that's consistently measured under the same conditions provides reliable change data even if the absolute body fat % is off by 2-3 points.

Interpreting Body Composition for Athletes

Body Fat Ranges by Sport Context

Context Males Females Notes
Essential fat3-5%12-15%Below this = health risk. Never target below essential fat.
Competitive combat sport6-12%14-20%Competition lean. Not sustainable year-round for most.
Training / off-season10-18%18-25%Sustainable, supports training and recovery.
General athletic10-20%18-28%Wide range depending on sport demands.

These are ranges, not targets. Individual athletes may perform optimally outside these ranges. Performance and health indicators matter more than hitting a number.

Critical principle: Body fat percentage is a data point, not a goal. An athlete's optimal body composition is the one at which they perform best, recover well, maintain hormonal health, and are psychologically healthy. Chasing a specific body fat number without regard for these factors leads to RED-S, disordered eating, and impaired performance.

The Monitoring Cadence Tradeoff

More data is not always better. There's a tradeoff between data collection and athlete burden:

  • Too much tracking: Daily food logging + daily weigh-ins + daily wellness surveys + weekly body comp = exhausting. The athlete burns out on tracking and stops doing any of it. This is especially true for athletes with disordered eating tendencies — excessive tracking can fuel obsessive behaviors.
  • Too little tracking: Monthly check-ins with no data between = flying blind. You can't course-correct if you don't know what's happening.
  • The sweet spot: Daily scale weight + daily wellness score (takes 30 seconds). Weekly photo food log (more sustainable than calorie counting for most athletes). Body comp every 4-8 weeks. Adjust based on the athlete's personality and relationship with tracking.

Adherence Strategies

The best nutrition plan is worthless if the athlete doesn't follow it. Adherence is the #1 predictor of outcome — not the macronutrient ratio, not the meal timing, not the supplement stack.

Why Athletes Don't Adhere

Common Adherence Barriers

Barrier Signal Solution
Plan is too complex"I can't keep track of everything"Simplify. Start with 2-3 changes, not a complete overhaul. Progress over time.
Doesn't match lifestyle"I can't eat this at work/on the road"Design around their actual schedule and food access. Ask where they eat, what's available, what they enjoy.
All-or-nothing thinking"I messed up my diet so I'll start over Monday"Reframe: one off-plan meal in a week of good nutrition is 95% adherence. Progress, not perfection.
Doesn't understand why"Why do I need to eat more on training days?"Educate. Athletes who understand the science behind recommendations are more likely to follow them.
Social/cultural pressure"My teammates all eat fast food after practice"Build flexibility into the plan. Teach best-choice strategies for real-world situations instead of avoidance.

Evidence-Based Adherence Techniques

  1. Start with what they're already eating. Don't throw out their entire diet and replace it. Identify the 2-3 highest-impact changes (e.g., "add protein to breakfast" or "bring a snack to afternoon training") and build from there.
  2. Use food preferences, not food rules. "You need to eat chicken and rice" is a rule. "What protein sources do you enjoy?" is a conversation. Plans built around foods the athlete actually likes are dramatically more adherent.
  3. Make the default option the healthy option. Meal prep removes decision-making. Pre-portioned snacks in the gym bag ensure fueling happens. The environment matters more than willpower.
  4. Check in frequently but briefly. A 5-minute weekly check-in ("How's the plan going? What's been hard? What's working?") maintains accountability without being burdensome.
  5. Celebrate process, not just outcomes. "You hit your protein target 6 out of 7 days" is a win worth recognizing, even if the scale didn't move that week.

Scope of Practice and When to Refer

Knowing your scope is as important as knowing your science. Nutrition practitioners encounter situations that require referral to other professionals.

When to Refer

Situation Refer To Your Role
Suspected eating disorderPsychologist/psychiatrist specializing in eating disorders + physicianIdentify warning signs, maintain supportive nutrition relationship, do NOT attempt to treat the disorder yourself.
RED-S indicatorsSports medicine physicianScreen and flag, begin addressing energy availability, coordinate with medical team on return-to-play.
Abnormal bloodworkPhysician or endocrinologistOrder or review bloodwork within your scope, flag abnormalities, adjust nutrition, refer for medical follow-up.
Mental health concernsSport psychologist or licensed therapistYou may notice mood changes, anxiety, or depression through your regular check-ins. Refer, don't counsel.
Acute injury nutritionCollaborate with athletic trainer / physiotherapistAdjust nutrition for recovery (higher protein, adequate calories, anti-inflammatory focus). Don't direct rehab protocols.
Weight cut beyond safe limitsRefuse and explain. Involve the coach and, if necessary, the athletic commission.Your professional obligation is to the athlete's health. Never facilitate a cut you believe is dangerous.

The most important skill in this list is saying no. If an athlete or coach asks you to facilitate something you believe is harmful — a dangerous weight cut, extreme caloric restriction, or ignoring clear RED-S indicators — your professional responsibility is to refuse, explain why, and document the conversation. Compliance with a harmful request is not "being a team player." It's malpractice.

Building Your Practice Workflow

The Consultation Cycle

  1. Initial Assessment (60-90 min): 7-domain intake, body comp baseline, goal setting, identify red flags, explain your approach. The athlete should leave understanding WHAT you'll do and WHY.
  2. Plan Delivery (within 3-5 days): Personalized nutrition plan with day-type macros, meal timing around training, supplement recommendations, and monitoring schedule. Start simple — 2-3 key changes first.
  3. Weekly Check-ins (15-30 min): Review weight trend, dietary adherence, training quality, subjective wellness. Troubleshoot barriers. Make small adjustments. Celebrate wins.
  4. Monthly Review (30-45 min): Body comp reassessment. Review adherence trends. Adjust macros if the plan isn't producing expected results. Introduce next-level changes if the fundamentals are solid.
  5. Phase Transitions: When training phases shift (base → build → peak → taper → competition → recovery), proactively adjust the nutrition plan. Don't wait for the athlete to ask — anticipate the change and communicate it before the phase starts.
  6. Post-Competition Debrief: What worked? What didn't? What would you change? Document for future camps. This data compounds — each camp is better than the last.

Documentation and Professional Practice

  • Document every consultation. Date, duration, topics covered, recommendations made, athlete response. This protects you professionally and creates a record that improves continuity of care.
  • Use standardized templates. A consistent intake form, check-in form, and progress report template saves time and ensures nothing is missed.
  • Maintain confidentiality. Athlete nutrition and body composition data is personal health information. Don't share it with coaches, teammates, or media without explicit athlete consent. In team settings, clarify upfront what information is shared with coaching staff and what stays between you and the athlete.
  • Stay within your credential. The scope of nutrition counseling varies by state and credential. Registered Dietitians have the broadest scope. Certified nutrition coaches have a narrower scope. Know your state's laws and your credential's boundaries.

Key Takeaways

  1. Assessment is the foundation. A structured 7-domain intake captures the information you need and surfaces red flags. Don't skip it.
  2. Screen for RED-S in every athlete. Low energy availability affects both sexes, all sports, and causes cascading health consequences. Know the warning signs and refer when indicated.
  3. Body composition is a data point, not a goal. Use appropriate methods, standardize conditions, track trends. Never chase a number at the expense of health or performance.
  4. Adherence beats perfection. A simple plan followed consistently outperforms an optimal plan followed sporadically. Design around the athlete's real life, not your ideal scenario.
  5. Know when to refer. Eating disorders, RED-S, abnormal bloodwork, and mental health concerns require other professionals. Your job is to screen, flag, and coordinate — not to treat everything yourself.
  6. Document everything. For continuity of care, professional protection, and the compounding value of data across multiple training cycles.

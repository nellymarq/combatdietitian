Introduction

The global sports nutrition supplement market exceeds $15 billion annually. Most of it is waste. The gap between what's marketed and what's supported by evidence is enormous — and for drug-tested athletes, the gap between what's legal and what's contaminated can end a career.

This module teaches you how to evaluate supplement claims critically, screen products for safety, assess dose adequacy, and make evidence-based recommendations for your athletes. You'll learn which supplements actually work, which are banned, and which are a waste of money.

The Evidence Hierarchy for Supplements

Not all evidence is equal. When evaluating a supplement claim, assess where the evidence comes from:

Evidence Hierarchy (Strongest to Weakest)

  1. Systematic Reviews & Meta-Analyses — Multiple RCTs pooled and analyzed. The strongest evidence. (e.g., ISSN position stands, Cochrane reviews)
  2. Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) — Single studies with placebo control and randomization. Strong but can have conflicting results across studies.
  3. Cohort & Observational Studies — Show associations, not causation. Useful for safety data but not for efficacy claims.
  4. Case Reports & Expert Opinion — Individual anecdotes or clinical experience. Starting points, not conclusions.
  5. Manufacturer Claims & Testimonials — Marketing, not science. The FDA does not pre-approve supplement efficacy claims. "Clinically proven" on a label means nothing without verifiable published research.

Rule of thumb: If a supplement's evidence is limited to the manufacturer's website and Instagram influencers, it's marketing. If it has multiple independent RCTs and an ISSN or IOC consensus position, it's evidence-based.

The "Big 5" Evidence-Based Supplements

Out of thousands of products on the market, only a handful have strong, consistent evidence for performance benefits. These five have the most robust research support:

1. Creatine Monohydrate

Evidence Level: Strong (ISSN Position Stand, 2017)
Effective Dose: 3-5g/day (maintenance) or 20g/day × 5-7 days (loading)
Mechanism: Increases intramuscular phosphocreatine stores, enhancing ATP regeneration during high-intensity efforts
WADA Status: Permitted
What it does: Improves performance in repeated high-intensity efforts (sprints, lifts, explosive movements). Increases lean mass by 1-2kg over 4-12 weeks (primarily water retention in muscle, not fat or new tissue). May improve recovery between bouts.
Who benefits: Strength athletes, combat sport athletes, team sport athletes, anyone performing repeated explosive efforts. Less relevant for pure endurance athletes.
Important for combat sports: The 1-2kg weight gain from creatine loading can be problematic for weight-class athletes. Consider timing: load during off-season or early camp, taper off 4+ weeks before weigh-in to allow water weight to clear.

2. Caffeine

Evidence Level: Strong (ISSN Position Stand, 2021)
Effective Dose: 3-6 mg/kg body weight, 30-60 min before exercise
Mechanism: Adenosine receptor antagonist — reduces perceived exertion, increases alertness, may enhance fat oxidation
WADA Status: Permitted (monitored program — not prohibited but tracked)
What it does: Improves endurance performance (2-4% improvement), strength/power (small but significant), and cognitive function (reaction time, decision-making). One of the most well-researched ergogenic aids.
Dose matters: For an 80kg athlete, 3-6 mg/kg = 240-480 mg. A standard coffee is ~80-100 mg caffeine. More is not better — doses above 6 mg/kg increase side effects (anxiety, GI distress, tachycardia) without additional performance benefit.
Tolerance: Regular caffeine consumers may need the higher end of the dose range. A caffeine "washout" (7-14 days reduced intake) before competition can restore sensitivity.

3. Beta-Alanine

Evidence Level: Strong (Saunders et al., 2017 meta-analysis)
Effective Dose: 3.2-6.4g/day, split into smaller doses (0.8-1.6g per dose to reduce paresthesia)
Mechanism: Increases intramuscular carnosine, which buffers hydrogen ions during glycolysis
WADA Status: Permitted
What it does: Improves performance in efforts lasting 1-4 minutes — exactly the range of combat sport rounds. The buffering effect delays the onset of fatigue from acid accumulation during sustained high-intensity work.
Loading period: Takes 4+ weeks of daily supplementation to saturate muscle carnosine stores. Not an acute performance enhancer — must be taken consistently.
Side effect: Paresthesia (tingling/itching sensation on skin) is harmless and dose-dependent. Splitting into smaller doses throughout the day minimizes this.

4. Sodium Bicarbonate

Evidence Level: Moderate-Strong (Carr et al., 2011 meta-analysis)
Effective Dose: 0.2-0.3g/kg, 60-90 min before exercise (or serial loading: 0.5g/kg/day for 3-5 days)
Mechanism: Extracellular buffer — increases blood bicarbonate, enhancing removal of hydrogen ions from muscle
WADA Status: Permitted
What it does: Works synergistically with beta-alanine (intracellular buffer + extracellular buffer). Benefits efforts in the 1-7 minute range — highly relevant for combat sports.
GI risk: The major limitation. Acute doses commonly cause nausea, bloating, and diarrhea. Serial loading over 3-5 days reduces GI issues. Enteric-coated capsules may also help. Always trial in training before competition.

5. Nitrate (Beetroot Juice)

Evidence Level: Moderate-Strong (Jones et al., 2018)
Effective Dose: 6-8 mmol nitrate (400-500 mL beetroot juice), 2-3 hours before exercise
Mechanism: Converted to nitric oxide, which improves blood flow, reduces oxygen cost of exercise, and enhances muscle contractile efficiency
WADA Status: Permitted
What it does: Improves exercise efficiency (lower oxygen cost at a given intensity), time to exhaustion, and repeated sprint performance. Benefits are most pronounced in recreational and moderately trained athletes; elite athletes show smaller improvements.
Practical note: Concentrated beetroot shots (e.g., Beet It, BeetElite) provide standardized nitrate doses in a small volume. Avoid mouthwash before beetroot — the oral bacteria that convert nitrate to nitrite are essential for the supplement to work.

Banned Substance Screening

The WADA Prohibited List

The World Anti-Doping Agency publishes an annually updated Prohibited List categorizing banned substances and methods. Key categories relevant to supplement contamination:

WADA Categories Most Commonly Found in Supplements

Category Examples Common Source
S1: Anabolic AgentsTestosterone, nandrolone, DHEA, SARMs"Testosterone boosters," prohormones, contaminated protein powders
S3: Beta-2 AgonistsClenbuterol, higenamineFat burners, pre-workouts
S4: Hormone & Metabolic ModulatorsSERMs (tamoxifen), aromatase inhibitors"PCT" products, anti-estrogen supplements
S5: Diuretics & Masking AgentsHydrochlorothiazide, probenecidWeight loss supplements, "water pills"
S6: StimulantsDMAA, DMHA, ephedrine, methylhexanaminePre-workouts, fat burners, energy supplements

The contamination problem is real. Multiple studies have found that 10-25% of supplements contain undeclared prohibited substances (Geyer et al., 2004; Martinez-Sanz et al., 2017). The athlete bears full responsibility for what they consume — "I didn't know it was in there" is not a defense under WADA's strict liability principle.

Third-Party Certification Programs

Third-party testing reduces — but does not eliminate — contamination risk:

Certification Programs

Program Tests For Limitation
NSF Certified for Sport270+ banned substances, label accuracy, contaminantsOnly tests products that brands submit; not all products/batches tested
Informed SportWADA prohibited list substances per batchEvery batch tested before market release (stronger than NSF per-batch)
BSCG (Banned Substance Control Group)WADA + FDA banned drugs + other substancesSmaller program, fewer certified products
ConsumerLabLabel accuracy, purity, potencyQuality testing, not banned substance testing specifically

Recommended hierarchy for drug-tested athletes:

  1. Informed Sport certified (batch-tested) — highest confidence
  2. NSF Certified for Sport — strong, widely available
  3. BSCG certified — good, smaller catalog
  4. No certification — higher risk. Use only if the ingredient is clearly single-compound (e.g., pure creatine monohydrate from a reputable manufacturer)

Assessing Dose Adequacy

A supplement can contain the right ingredient but the wrong dose. "Pixie dusting" — including an ingredient at a fraction of its effective dose so it can appear on the label — is endemic in the supplement industry.

The Dose Adequacy Check

For every supplement an athlete takes, ask three questions:

  1. What is the active ingredient? Look past the brand name. "MuscleMax 5000" means nothing — what's the actual compound?
  2. What dose is in the product? Read the Supplement Facts panel. Check per-serving dose, not per-container. Watch for proprietary blends where individual ingredient doses are hidden.
  3. What does the evidence say the effective dose is? Compare the product's dose to published effective dose ranges from peer-reviewed research. If the product provides 500mg of an ingredient whose effective dose is 3,000mg, it's underdosed.

Red Flags: When to Reject a Product

Automatic Red Flags

  1. Proprietary blend with hidden individual doses. If you can't see how much of each ingredient is in the product, you can't verify the dose is effective or safe. "Proprietary Blend 5,000mg" with 10 ingredients listed = you have no idea how much of anything is in there.
  2. Claims that sound too good to be true. "Increases testosterone 300%," "burns fat while you sleep," "clinically proven to add 10lbs of muscle in 4 weeks." These claims are illegal under FTC rules and indicate a company willing to break regulations.
  3. No third-party certification for drug-tested athletes. If the athlete is subject to WADA, USADA, or any drug testing, uncertified products carry unacceptable risk.
  4. Contains ingredients with names that mimic banned substances. DMAA, DMHA, methylhexanamine, 1,3-dimethylamylamine — these are prohibited stimulants that appear in pre-workouts. Any product containing these is an automatic fail.
  5. Manufactured in a facility that also produces prohibited substances. Cross-contamination risk. Look for GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) certification and check if the manufacturer also makes products containing steroids, SARMs, or other banned compounds.
  6. Only available from a single online source or social media seller. Legitimate supplements are sold through established retail channels. Products only available through Instagram DMs or a single website are high risk.

Building a Supplement Protocol for Athletes

A responsible supplement protocol follows the "food first" principle — supplements fill gaps that diet can't close, not replace whole food nutrition. Here's a framework:

Step 1: Assess the Need

  • Is there a documented deficiency (blood work showing low vitamin D, iron, etc.)?
  • Is there a performance demand the supplement can address (creatine for power, caffeine for alertness)?
  • Can the need be met through food instead?

Step 2: Check the Evidence

  • Does the ingredient have RCT-level evidence for the claimed benefit?
  • Is the evidence from the relevant population (athletes, not sedentary adults)?
  • What's the ISSN, IOC, or ACSM position?

Step 3: Screen the Product

  • Third-party certified? (NSF, Informed Sport, BSCG)
  • Dose adequate? (Compare label to effective dose range)
  • Any red flags? (Proprietary blends, suspicious claims, uncertified)
  • WADA status? (Check GlobalDRO for medications, WADA Prohibited List for ingredients)

Step 4: Implement and Monitor

  • Introduce one supplement at a time (so you can attribute effects)
  • Trial in training before competition (especially caffeine, bicarbonate, beetroot)
  • Set a review date — is the supplement doing what you expected?
  • Discontinue anything that isn't clearly beneficial

Key Takeaways

  1. Most supplements don't work. Of thousands of products, only a handful have strong evidence: creatine, caffeine, beta-alanine, sodium bicarbonate, and nitrate (beetroot) lead the evidence base.
  2. 10-25% of supplements contain undeclared prohibited substances. Third-party certification (NSF, Informed Sport) reduces but doesn't eliminate this risk. For drug-tested athletes, uncertified products are unacceptable.
  3. Dose matters more than the ingredient name. A product containing the right ingredient at the wrong dose is a waste of money. Always compare label doses to evidence-based effective dose ranges.
  4. Proprietary blends are a red flag. If you can't see individual ingredient doses, you can't verify the product is effective or safe.
  5. Food first, always. Supplements fill gaps — they don't replace a well-structured nutrition plan. An athlete with a poor diet and excellent supplements will underperform an athlete with a great diet and no supplements.
  6. The athlete bears strict liability. Under WADA rules, the athlete is responsible for everything they consume. "I didn't know" is not a defense. Your role as a practitioner is to minimize this risk.

Introduction

A fight camp is a structured 8-12 week preparation period where training volume, intensity, and nutritional demands shift dramatically from week to week. Nutrition during fight camp serves two competing goals: fuel training performance and progressively manage body weight toward the target weight class. Mismanaging this balance — under-fueling and compromising training quality, or over-fueling and leaving too much weight for fight week — is one of the most common errors in combat sport nutrition.

This module breaks fight camp into distinct phases and provides specific macronutrient targets, meal timing strategies, and supplement protocols for each phase.

Phase 1: Early Camp (Weeks 8-12 to Week 5)

Early camp emphasizes building the aerobic base, establishing training volume, and beginning gradual body composition improvement. This is where chronic weight management work happens — the goal is progressive fat loss without compromising training quality or recovery.

Caloric Targets

A moderate caloric deficit of 300-500 kcal/day below maintenance supports a rate of weight loss of approximately 0.5-0.7% body mass per week — the threshold below which lean mass preservation is maximized (Garthe et al., 2011, International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism). For a 77kg fighter with a maintenance intake of 3,000 kcal, the target is 2,500-2,700 kcal/day.

Phase 2: Mid Camp (Weeks 5-3)

Mid camp is the highest training intensity phase. Sparring volume peaks, sport-specific conditioning is at maximum, and the athlete is sharpening technical skills under fatigue. Nutritionally, this is the phase where fueling training quality must take priority over aggressive weight loss.

If the athlete is on track with body composition targets from early camp, the caloric deficit can be held steady at 300-500 kcal/day. If behind schedule, the temptation is to increase the deficit — but this is the worst time to under-fuel. Research by Morton et al. (2018) in the British Journal of Sports Medicine demonstrated that energy deficiency during high training loads increases injury risk, impairs immune function, and reduces training adaptation.

Meal Timing for Two-a-Day Sessions

When fighters train twice daily (common in mid camp), nutrient timing becomes critical for recovery between sessions:

  • • Pre-AM session (2-3 hrs before): 1-2 g/kg carbs + 0.3 g/kg protein (e.g., oatmeal, eggs, fruit)
  • • Post-AM / Pre-PM window (within 2 hrs): 1-1.2 g/kg carbs + 0.3-0.4 g/kg protein — this meal serves double duty as recovery from session 1 and fuel for session 2
  • • Post-PM session (within 2 hrs): 0.8-1 g/kg carbs + 0.3-0.4 g/kg protein
  • • Evening meal: Protein-focused (0.3-0.4 g/kg) with vegetables; lower carbs if caloric budget is tight

Phase 3: Peak Week (Week 2)

Peak week marks the transition from training-focused to weight-focused nutrition. Training volume begins to taper, which naturally reduces caloric expenditure. The athlete's caloric intake can decrease to match the reduced training load without creating an excessive physiological deficit.

Key peak week adjustments:

  • Carbohydrate reduction: Drop to 2-3 g/kg/day as training volume decreases. This begins depleting glycogen stores (each gram of glycogen stores ~3g water, so glycogen depletion contributes to scale weight loss).
  • Fiber management: Begin transitioning toward lower-fiber food choices (white rice instead of brown, peeled vegetables, white bread). This reduces gut residue weight — a meaningful contributor to scale weight that is often overlooked.
  • Protein maintained: Keep at 2.0-2.4 g/kg. Lean mass preservation remains the priority even as total calories drop.
  • Water loading initiation: Begin the water loading protocol (covered in Module 3) during peak week.

Phase 4: Fight Week (Final 5-7 Days)

Fight week nutrition is almost entirely about making weight safely while preserving as much performance capacity as possible. Training is minimal (light technical work only), and the nutritional focus shifts to acute weight manipulation strategies that are covered in detail in Modules 3 and 4.

Managing Hunger During Caloric Restriction

Hunger management is a practical skill that separates successful fight camp nutrition from failed plans. Strategies supported by research (Leidy et al., 2015, Advances in Nutrition):

  • High-protein meals: Protein is the most satiating macronutrient. Front-loading protein at each meal (30-40g) reduces subsequent hunger.
  • Volume eating: Prioritize low-calorie, high-volume foods (leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, broth-based soups) to maintain stomach distension without caloric cost.
  • Meal frequency: 4-5 smaller meals often manages hunger better than 2-3 large meals during a deficit, though individual preference varies.
  • Caffeine: Moderate caffeine intake (200-400mg/day) has mild appetite-suppressive effects and supports training performance. Avoid after 2 PM to protect sleep.
  • Sleep hygiene: Sleep restriction increases ghrelin (hunger hormone) and decreases leptin (satiety hormone). Prioritizing 7-9 hours of sleep is a hunger management strategy (Spiegel et al., 2004, Annals of Internal Medicine).

Supplement Protocols by Phase

Phase-Specific Supplement Recommendations

  • All phases: Creatine monohydrate 3-5g/day (discontinue 5-7 days before weigh-in due to water retention — approximately 1-2kg), vitamin D (2,000-4,000 IU/day if deficient), omega-3 (2-3g EPA+DHA/day for anti-inflammatory support during high training loads).
  • Early/Mid camp: Caffeine 3-6 mg/kg pre-training for performance. Beta-alanine 3.2-6.4g/day (split doses) for buffering capacity during high-intensity sparring blocks.
  • Peak week: Discontinue creatine. Maintain caffeine but consider reducing dose to preserve sensitivity for fight night. Begin electrolyte manipulation supplements as per Module 3 protocol.
  • Fight week: Discontinue all supplements that contribute to water retention. Caffeine may be used strategically for appetite suppression during the final cut days.

Key Takeaways

  1. Fight camp nutrition has distinct phases — a single diet plan for the entire camp will either under-fuel peak training or fail to manage weight adequately.
  2. Rate of weight loss should not exceed 0.5-0.7% body mass per week during the chronic phase to preserve lean mass and training quality.
  3. Mid camp is the worst time to increase caloric restriction — training intensity peaks and under-fueling increases injury risk and impairs adaptation.
  4. Meal timing around two-a-day sessions is critical: the post-AM/pre-PM meal serves double duty for recovery and fueling.
  5. Hunger management is a practical skill — high-protein meals, volume eating, adequate sleep, and strategic caffeine use all contribute to adherence.

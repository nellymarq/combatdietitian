Introduction

In Module 1, we established that different training sessions tax different energy systems — and that the fuel those systems use is determined by intensity and duration. The logical next step: if training demands change across a training cycle, nutrition should change with them.

This is macronutrient periodization — the systematic manipulation of carbohydrate, protein, and fat intake to match the demands of each training phase, each day type, and each session. It's the difference between a static meal plan that never changes and a dynamic nutrition strategy that evolves with the athlete's training.

This module covers the evidence behind periodization, the practical frameworks for implementing it, and the day-to-day decision-making that separates effective practitioners from meal plan factories.

Why Periodize? The Case Against Static Nutrition

Most athletes eat the same thing every day. Most meal plans prescribe the same macros Monday through Sunday. This is a problem because training is not the same every day.

Consider a typical training week for a combat sport athlete:

Sample Combat Sport Training Week

MondayS&C (heavy)MMA drillingHigh
TuesdaySparringConditioningVery High
WednesdayActive recoveryTechnical drillingLow
ThursdayS&C (moderate)SparringHigh

Tuesday (sparring + conditioning) and Sunday (rest) have completely different metabolic demands. Prescribing the same 2,800 calories and 350g carbohydrates for both days is nutritional malpractice — Tuesday needs more fuel, and Sunday needs less. This isn't controversial; it's physics.

The seminal framework for this concept is "Fuel for the Work Required" (Impey et al., 2018) — the idea that carbohydrate availability should be planned around the demands of each specific training session, not averaged across the week.

Training Phases: The Macro View

Before adjusting day-to-day nutrition, you need to understand the phase-level context. Most athletes train in structured phases (also called "blocks" or "mesocycles") that shift in focus over 3-6 weeks. Each phase has different nutritional demands.

The key principle: nutrition should support what training is trying to achieve in each phase. During the base phase, the goal is building work capacity — nutrition supports high-volume training with adequate carbohydrate and calories. During taper, volume drops — nutrition should drop proportionally, not stay at base-phase levels (which would create an unintended surplus).

Day-Type Periodization: The Micro View

Within each phase, daily nutrition should fluctuate based on what that day's training demands. This is the practical, day-to-day application of periodization that athletes actually feel.

The Four Day Types

Day Type Carbs (relative) Calories (relative) Fat (relative) Protein
Training (hard)100%100%100%Constant
Competition / Game125%110%80%Constant
Rest60%85%125%Constant
Travel75%90%110%Constant

Scaling factors based on Impey et al. (2018) and Burke et al. (2011). Training day = baseline. All other days scale from that baseline.

Notice that protein remains constant across all day types. This is intentional. Protein serves structural and recovery functions that don't fluctuate with training load the way energy demands do. The current evidence supports 1.6-2.2g/kg/day for most athletes (Morton et al., 2018), with the upper end (2.2-2.8g/kg) appropriate during caloric restriction or high-damage training like sparring.

Carbohydrates are the primary lever. They change the most between day types because glycogen demand is directly tied to training volume and intensity. Fat fills the remaining caloric gap — when carbs go down on rest days, fat goes up for satiety and hormonal health.

Worked Example

Let's apply this to an 80kg combat sport athlete with a training-day baseline of 2,800 kcal:

The athlete eats 420 fewer calories on rest days than training days — almost entirely from reduced carbohydrates. This is not a "diet." It's matching fuel to demand. On rest days, glycogen resynthesis needs are minimal, so carbohydrate intake drops. On competition days, carbs increase 25% to ensure full glycogen stores and peri-competition fuel.

Carbohydrate Periodization Strategies

Beyond day-type scaling, there are specific carbohydrate periodization strategies supported by research. These are tools — not dogma. Use them when the context calls for it.

Train Low, Compete High

The foundational strategy from Burke et al. (2011): deliberately perform selected training sessions with reduced carbohydrate availability to enhance metabolic adaptations (increased fat oxidation, mitochondrial biogenesis), then ensure full glycogen stores for competition.

Important nuance: "Train low" does not mean "always train low." It means strategically training with low glycogen for specific sessions — typically low-intensity, steady-state aerobic work where the adaptation goal is metabolic efficiency, not maximal performance. High-intensity sessions (sparring, intervals, heavy S&C) should always be fueled with adequate glycogen. Training high-intensity sessions in a glycogen-depleted state increases injury risk, impairs technique, and blunts the intended training stimulus.

When to Train Low vs. Train High

Train LOW (reduced carbs)

  • • Morning easy cardio (fasted or low-carb breakfast)
  • • Low-intensity technical drilling
  • • Active recovery sessions
  • • Second session of a two-a-day (after "sleep low" — see below)

Train HIGH (full carbs)

  • • Sparring and hard drilling
  • • High-intensity intervals / conditioning
  • • Heavy S&C sessions
  • • Any session where injury risk exists
  • • Competition

Sleep Low

A practical implementation of "train low": perform an afternoon/evening training session, then eat a low-carbohydrate dinner (high protein + vegetables + fat). Sleep with reduced glycogen. The next morning's session is performed in a glycogen-depleted state — typically an easy aerobic session that benefits from enhanced fat oxidation signaling.

The evidence (Marquet et al., 2016) shows that sleep-low strategies improved 10km running performance and cycling efficiency in trained athletes compared to the same total caloric and carbohydrate intake distributed evenly. The total weekly intake was identical — only the timing changed.

Protocol:

  1. PM session: high-intensity (sparring, intervals) — fueled normally
  2. Post-PM meal: high protein, low carb (e.g., chicken + salad + avocado, NOT chicken + rice)
  3. Sleep with reduced glycogen
  4. AM session next day: low-intensity aerobic (fasted or minimal carbs)
  5. Post-AM meal: restore carbohydrates (oats, fruit, rice) to prepare for the day's main session

Carb Loading for Competition

For events lasting >90 minutes at high intensity, carbohydrate loading in the 24-48 hours before competition maximizes muscle glycogen stores. The modern protocol (Bussau et al., 2002) is simpler than the classic 6-day depletion/loading protocol:

  • 24-48 hours before competition: increase carbohydrate intake to 8-12g/kg/day
  • Reduce training volume (you're tapering anyway)
  • Choose easily digestible, low-fiber, low-fat carbohydrate sources: white rice, pasta, bread, sports drinks, fruit juice, pancakes
  • Expect 1-2kg of weight gain from glycogen and associated water storage (3g water per 1g glycogen) — this is performance weight, not body fat

For combat sport athletes managing weight: carb loading is typically done after weigh-in as part of the rehydration and refueling protocol (covered in the Combat Sports specialization track). The timing consideration — loading AFTER a weight cut vs. loading BEFORE a competition — is a unique challenge in weight-class sports.

Protein: The Constant Variable

While carbohydrates fluctuate, protein remains stable. Here's why, and how much is enough.

How Much Protein?

Protein Recommendations by Context

Context g/kg/day Rationale
General athletic population1.6-2.0Sufficient for MPS and recovery (Morton et al., 2018)
High-volume training / combat sports2.0-2.4Higher tissue damage from contact, longer sessions
During caloric deficit (weight cut)2.2-2.8Preserves lean mass during energy restriction (Helms et al., 2014)
Recovery from injury2.0-2.5Supports tissue repair, offsets immobilization-induced muscle loss

Protein Distribution Matters

Total daily protein is the primary driver, but distribution across meals optimizes muscle protein synthesis (MPS). The evidence supports:

  • 0.3-0.4g/kg per meal, distributed across 4-5 meals/snacks (Schoenfeld & Aragon, 2018)
  • Pre-sleep protein (30-40g casein or mixed protein) supports overnight MPS (Snijders et al., 2015)
  • Post-training protein within 2 hours (the "anabolic window" is wider than the old 30-minute myth, but sooner is still slightly better than later)
  • For an 80kg athlete at 2.0g/kg = 160g/day, this means ~32-40g per meal across 4-5 eating occasions

Fat: The Flexible Variable

Fat fills the caloric gap after protein and carbohydrates are set. It's the flexible variable — adjusted inversely to carbohydrates across day types.

Minimum Fat Intake

Fat should never drop below 0.8-1.0g/kg/day (or ~20% of total calories) for any sustained period. Below this threshold:

  • Hormonal function is impaired (testosterone, estrogen production requires dietary fat)
  • Fat-soluble vitamin absorption decreases (A, D, E, K)
  • Cell membrane integrity is compromised
  • Satiety drops dramatically (increasing adherence risk)

On rest days, fat naturally increases as carbohydrates decrease. This is beneficial — fat provides satiety on days when caloric intake is lower, and the body's fat oxidation capacity is highest at rest and during low-intensity activity.

On competition days, fat decreases to make room for increased carbohydrates and to reduce gastrointestinal risk — high-fat meals before competition are a common cause of GI distress during performance.

Session-Specific Nutrition Timing

Within each day, meal timing relative to training sessions further optimizes fuel availability and recovery.

Pre-Training (2-3 hours before)

  • 1-2g/kg carbohydrates (easily digestible — rice, oats, banana, toast)
  • 0.3g/kg protein
  • Low fat, low fiber (reduce GI risk during training)
  • For sparring days: lighter meal, further from session (GI distress during sparring is common with a heavy stomach)

Intra-Training (during sessions >60 min)

  • 30-60g/hr carbohydrate for sessions over 60 minutes at moderate-high intensity
  • Sports drink, gel, or banana — simple, fast-digesting sources
  • Carbohydrate mouth rinse: even rinsing a carb solution in the mouth (without swallowing) activates brain receptors that reduce perceived exertion. Useful for sessions where stomach fullness is a concern (Carter et al., 2004).

Post-Training (within 2 hours)

  • 0.3-0.4g/kg protein (whey, chicken, eggs, fish — leucine-rich sources preferred)
  • 1-1.2g/kg carbohydrate (rapid glycogen resynthesis, especially if another session follows within 8 hours)
  • After sparring specifically: emphasize anti-inflammatory foods — omega-3 rich sources (salmon, walnuts), polyphenol-rich fruits (berries, tart cherry juice). Sparring creates more tissue damage than other session types.

Common Mistakes

Periodization Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Training low for every session. Some coaches interpret "train low" as "always restrict carbs." This impairs high-intensity training quality, increases injury risk, and can lead to Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S). Reserve train-low for specific, planned low-intensity sessions.
  2. Ignoring the rest day. Rest day nutrition matters. Protein should remain constant (recovery happens on rest days). Carbs should decrease (no glycogen demand). Many athletes eat their biggest meals on rest days out of boredom — awareness corrects this.
  3. Not scaling with the phase. An athlete in a taper phase eating base-phase calories will gain unintended weight. Scale total intake with training volume changes across phases.
  4. Over-complicating too early. For an athlete who doesn't track anything, start with day-type scaling (training vs rest day carbs) before introducing sleep-low or train-low strategies. Progression: consistent eating → day-type scaling → advanced periodization.
  5. Cutting fat too low on high-carb days. On competition or high-training days, carbs are high and fat is reduced. But don't drop below 0.8g/kg/day sustained. If the math pushes fat that low, you may need to increase total calories rather than squeeze fat further.

Key Takeaways

  1. Fuel for the work required. Daily nutrition should reflect the day's training demands, not a static weekly average.
  2. Carbohydrates are the primary periodization lever. They fluctuate with training load. Protein stays constant. Fat fills the gap.
  3. Phase context sets the ceiling. Day-type adjustments happen within the caloric range defined by the current training phase.
  4. "Train low, compete high" is a targeted strategy, not a lifestyle. Low-carb training is reserved for specific low-intensity sessions. High-intensity and skill work should always be fueled.
  5. Protein distribution across 4-5 meals at 0.3-0.4g/kg per meal optimizes muscle protein synthesis throughout the day.
  6. Start simple. Training-day vs rest-day carb scaling is the single highest-impact periodization change for most athletes. Advanced strategies build on top of this foundation.

Introduction

The period between weigh-in and competition is where fights are won or lost nutritionally. An athlete who cuts 4% of body mass through water manipulation and then fails to rehydrate effectively enters the cage, ring, or mat with impaired cardiovascular function, reduced reaction time, diminished strength, and compromised thermoregulation. Conversely, an athlete who executes a systematic, evidence-based rehydration and fueling protocol can recover 95-100% of lost body mass and compete at or near full performance capacity.

This module covers oral rehydration science, body-weight-scaled rehydration targets, glycogen replenishment, pre-fight meal composition, caffeine timing, and intra-competition fueling.

Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) Composition

Plain water is a poor rehydration fluid after a weight cut. It lacks the sodium required for optimal intestinal absorption and can dilute plasma sodium concentration, triggering further urinary losses before adequate rehydration is achieved. This is the physiological basis for oral rehydration solutions.

The WHO oral rehydration solution formula — originally developed for cholera treatment — provides the optimal sodium-glucose ratio for maximal intestinal water absorption via the SGLT1 sodium-glucose cotransporter (Leiper, 2015, Nutrition Reviews). The key principle: glucose and sodium must be co-transported for water to follow osmotically across the intestinal epithelium.

ORS Composition for Post-Weigh-In Rehydration

Component Target per Liter Practical Source
Sodium40-80 mmol/LTable salt (1/4 - 1/2 tsp/L) or commercial ORS
Glucose75-135 mmol/LTable sugar, dextrose powder, or honey
Potassium20-25 mmol/LCoconut water, banana, or potassium salt
Osmolality200-270 mOsm/LHypotonic to isotonic for fastest absorption

Standard sports drinks (Gatorade, Powerade) provide 10-25 mmol/L sodium — insufficient for post-cut rehydration. Purpose-built ORS products (Pedialyte, Trioral, Drip Drop) or custom-mixed solutions are required.

Absorption rate: The small intestine can absorb approximately 600-800 mL of properly formulated ORS per hour (Gisolfi et al., 1998, Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise). This rate limits how quickly an athlete can rehydrate and must be factored into the recovery timeline. Drinking faster than this rate causes gastric distension, nausea, and potentially vomiting — which worsens dehydration.

Body-Weight-Scaled Rehydration Targets

The rehydration target is straightforward: replace 150% of the fluid lost during the weight cut (Shirreffs et al., 1996, Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise). The 150% factor accounts for ongoing urinary and respiratory losses during the recovery period.

Rehydration Calculation Example

Fight weight: 77.1 kg (weigh-in)
Pre-cut weight: 80.5 kg (baseline before water manipulation began)
Fluid deficit: 80.5 - 77.1 = 3.4 kg = 3.4 L
Rehydration target: 3.4 L × 1.5 = 5.1 L over the recovery period
At 600-800 mL/hr absorption: 6.4 - 8.5 hours to fully rehydrate

This timeline underscores why 24-hour weigh-ins provide a meaningful recovery advantage over same-day weigh-ins — the physiology simply cannot support full rehydration in less than 6-8 hours for a moderate cut.

Glycogen Replenishment Protocol

Glycogen stores were intentionally depleted during the weight cut (Module 3). Replenishment is critical because glycogen fuels the high-intensity efforts that determine fight outcomes — takedowns, combinations, scrambles, and submission attempts.

The evidence-based target for rapid glycogen resynthesis is 1.0-1.2 g carbohydrate/kg/hour for the first 4-6 hours post-weigh-in (Burke et al., 2017, Journal of Sports Sciences). High-glycemic carbohydrate sources maximize glycogen synthase activity.

Practical Glycogen Replenishment for an 80kg Fighter

  • Target: 80-96g carbohydrate per hour for 4-6 hours = 320-576g total
  • Source selection: White rice, white bread, rice cakes, potatoes (peeled), sports drinks, fruit juice, honey, dextrose-based supplements
  • Avoid high-fat and high-fiber foods during this window — they slow gastric emptying and reduce the rate of carbohydrate absorption and glycogen synthesis
  • Co-ingest protein: Adding 0.3-0.4g protein/kg per meal enhances glycogen resynthesis by stimulating insulin secretion (Ivy et al., 2002, Journal of Applied Physiology)

Pre-Fight Meal Timing and Composition

The pre-fight meal is the final significant nutrition input before competition. Timing and composition must balance glycogen top-off, gastrointestinal comfort, and practical logistics (travel to venue, warm-up schedule, commission check-in).

Caffeine Timing for Fight Night

Caffeine is one of the most well-supported ergogenic aids for combat sport performance, with demonstrated benefits for reaction time, power output, and perceived exertion (Pickering & Kiely, 2019, Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition). The effective dose for performance benefits is 3-6 mg/kg body weight, consumed 30-60 minutes before competition.

For an 80kg fighter, this means 240-480mg of caffeine. Key considerations:

  • Tolerance: Habitual caffeine users may need the higher end of the range. Athletes who reduced caffeine intake during fight week to preserve sensitivity may respond to lower doses.
  • GI tolerance: Caffeine on a depleted stomach can cause cramping and urgency. Always consume with food or a carbohydrate-containing drink.
  • Source consistency: Caffeine pills or measured liquid sources are preferred over coffee (variable caffeine content) for reliable dosing on fight night.
  • Fight card position: If the athlete's bout timing is uncertain (common in MMA), take caffeine once the bout order is confirmed, not hours in advance. Caffeine's half-life is 3-5 hours — early dosing may mean the peak effect has passed by fight time.

Between-Round and Between-Fight Fueling

Between-Round Nutrition (1-Minute Rest)

  • • Water: 150-250 mL (small sips, not gulps — gastric distension impairs performance)
  • • Carbohydrate mouth rinse: Swirl a 6-8% carbohydrate solution in the mouth for 5-10 seconds. Research shows this activates oral carbohydrate receptors that signal the brain to reduce perceived exertion, even without ingestion (Chambers et al., 2009, Journal of Physiology).
  • • Avoid: Large volumes of fluid, solid foods, or anything that requires chewing — the time is too short and the risk of aspiration during exertion is real.

Post-Fight Recovery Nutrition

After the fight, the priority shifts to recovery. Within the first 2 hours post-competition:

  • Fluid: Continue rehydration with sodium-containing fluids. Monitor urine color — target pale yellow.
  • Carbohydrate: 1-1.2 g/kg to begin glycogen restoration
  • Protein: 0.3-0.4 g/kg for muscle repair, particularly if the athlete sustained significant tissue damage
  • Anti-inflammatory foods: Tart cherry juice (480 mL) has demonstrated anti-inflammatory and recovery benefits in combat athletes (Quinlan & Hill, 2020, European Journal of Sport Science)

Key Takeaways

  1. Oral rehydration solutions outperform plain water and standard sports drinks — the sodium-glucose cotransport mechanism is essential for rapid fluid absorption.
  2. Rehydrate at 150% of fluid lost, limited by intestinal absorption (~600-800 mL/hr). A 3.4 kg cut requires 6-8+ hours for full recovery.
  3. Glycogen replenishment requires 1.0-1.2 g carbs/kg/hr from high-glycemic sources during the first 4-6 hours post-weigh-in.
  4. Pre-fight meal timing is 3-4 hours before with a smaller top-up at 1-2 hours. All foods must be familiar and rehearsed.
  5. Caffeine at 3-6 mg/kg, 30-60 minutes before the bout — timed to fight card position, not a fixed clock time.

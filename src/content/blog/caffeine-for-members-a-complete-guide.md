Caffeine is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance on Earth and, alongside creatine, one of the most evidence-backed ergogenic aids in sports nutrition. The International Olympic Committee, the ISSN, and the ACSM all recognize caffeine as a legitimate performance enhancer.

Yet many members use caffeine haphazardly — consuming inconsistent doses at inconsistent times, building tolerance that diminishes the effect, and disrupting sleep in the process.

## How Caffeine Enhances Performance

Caffeine's primary mechanism is adenosine receptor antagonism. By blocking adenosine (a neurotransmitter that promotes drowsiness), caffeine increases alertness, reduces perceived exertion, and delays fatigue.

The performance benefits are well-documented across multiple domains:

- **Endurance:** 2-6% improvement in time trial performance
- **Strength and power:** Modest improvements (2-5%) in peak force and repeated sprint ability
- **Cognitive function:** Improved reaction time, decision-making, and vigilance — particularly relevant for team sports
- **RPE reduction:** Athletes perceive the same workload as easier, allowing them to sustain higher intensities

## Optimal Dosing

The performance-enhancing dose of caffeine is **3-6 mg/kg body weight**, consumed **30-60 minutes before exercise.**

For a 70 kg member, that is 210-420 mg — roughly the equivalent of two to four cups of coffee.

Important nuances:

- **More is not better.** Doses above 6 mg/kg do not produce additional performance benefits but do increase the risk of side effects: anxiety, GI distress, tremor, and tachycardia.
- **Low doses work.** Research shows that even 2 mg/kg (140 mg for a 70 kg member — about one strong coffee) produces measurable performance effects with minimal side effects.
- **Individual variation is enormous.** Some members are responders who see large performance gains; others are non-responders. Genetics play a significant role.

## The Genetics of Caffeine Response

The CYP1A2 gene determines how quickly the liver metabolizes caffeine:

- **Fast metabolizers (AA genotype):** Clear caffeine quickly. Tend to experience the full performance benefit with minimal side effects.
- **Slow metabolizers (AC or CC genotype):** Process caffeine more slowly. May experience prolonged stimulation, sleep disruption, and less consistent performance effects.

Approximately 50% of the population carries the slow-metabolizer variant. This explains why caffeine "does not work" for some members and why others cannot tolerate it without jitters and insomnia.

## Timing Strategies

- **Pre-exercise (30-60 min before):** The standard protocol. Peak plasma concentration occurs at approximately 45-60 minutes post-ingestion.
- **During exercise:** For events lasting over two hours, caffeine consumed during the second half of the event (via gels, cola, or caffeine capsules) can provide a late-race boost.
- **Caffeine nap:** Consuming caffeine immediately before a 20-minute nap. Caffeine takes 20-30 minutes to reach peak levels, so the member wakes up as the caffeine kicks in. Effective for members with early-morning or late-night competitions.

## Habituation and Periodization

Regular caffeine consumption leads to tolerance — the same dose produces a smaller effect over time. This has led to the concept of caffeine periodization:

- **Withdraw caffeine** for 7-14 days before a major competition to resensitize adenosine receptors
- **Reintroduce** at the competition dose on race day for maximum effect

The downside: caffeine withdrawal produces headaches, fatigue, irritability, and decreased performance for three to five days. Athletes must plan withdrawal during a deload week, not during heavy training.

An alternative approach: maintain low daily intake (100-200 mg) and use higher doses (3-6 mg/kg) only on competition days. This maintains some tolerance while preserving a larger acute response.

## Caffeine Sources

- **Coffee:** 80-150 mg per 8 oz cup. Variable due to brewing method, bean type, and serving size.
- **Caffeine capsules/tablets:** Precise dosing. 100-200 mg per capsule. The most reliable option for members who need exact amounts.
- **Pre-workout supplements:** Highly variable (100-400+ mg). Always check the label.
- **Energy drinks:** 80-300 mg per can. Often contain other stimulants (taurine, guarana) that complicate the picture.
- **Caffeinated gels:** 25-50 mg per gel. Useful for during-exercise caffeine intake in endurance events.

## Practical Advice for Dietitians

- **Audit total daily caffeine** from all sources (coffee, tea, pre-workout, soda, chocolate)
- **Set a cutoff time** — no caffeine after noon to 1 PM for members with sleep concerns
- **Test during training** — never introduce a new caffeine protocol on race day
- **Consider genetics** — if an member reports caffeine sensitivity, jitters, or sleep disruption at moderate doses, they may be a slow metabolizer

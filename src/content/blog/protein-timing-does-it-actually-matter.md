If you have spent any time in a weight room, you have heard some version of the protein timing gospel: you must consume protein within 30 minutes of training or your gains are gone. The so-called "anabolic window" has driven more post-workout shake sales than any other concept in sports nutrition.

But what does the evidence actually say?

## The Anabolic Window — Revisited

The original research on post-exercise protein timing studied fasted subjects performing resistance training. In that specific context — training on an empty stomach — consuming protein shortly after exercise did produce a measurable benefit for muscle protein synthesis (MPS).

The problem is that most members do not train fasted. If you ate a mixed meal containing 30-40g of protein two to three hours before training, amino acids from that meal are still circulating in your bloodstream during and after the session. The urgency of immediate post-workout protein drops significantly.

A 2013 meta-analysis by Schoenfeld, Aragon, and Krieger examined 23 studies and concluded that **total daily protein intake was a far stronger predictor of hypertrophy and strength gains than timing alone.** When total protein was equated, the timing effect largely disappeared.

## What Actually Matters: Distribution

Where timing does appear to matter is in **protein distribution across the day.** Research consistently shows that spreading protein intake across four to five feedings — roughly every three to four hours — optimizes MPS more effectively than consuming the same total amount in one or two large meals.

The practical recommendation:

- **0.4-0.55 g/kg per meal**, spread across four meals, to hit the daily target of 1.6-2.2 g/kg
- Each feeding should reach the **leucine threshold** (approximately 2.5-3g of leucine, found in roughly 25-40g of high-quality protein)
- Avoid long gaps (greater than five hours) between protein-containing meals

## Pre-Sleep Protein

One timing strategy that does have consistent support is **pre-sleep protein intake.** Multiple studies have demonstrated that consuming 30-40g of casein protein before bed increases overnight MPS without affecting fat oxidation or next-morning appetite.

This is particularly relevant for members in a caloric surplus or those trying to maximize lean mass gain. The overnight period represents six to eight hours without amino acid availability — pre-sleep protein fills that gap.

## Practical Takeaways for Dietitians

- **Stop stressing about the 30-minute window.** If the member ate a meal before training, post-workout protein can happen within two hours without meaningful loss.
- **Focus on daily total and distribution.** Four evenly spaced protein feedings is the evidence-based target.
- **Consider pre-sleep casein** for members in hypertrophy or mass-gain phases.
- **For fasted training** (early morning sessions), post-workout protein does become more important. Prioritize a protein-rich meal or shake within an hour.

The protein timing conversation has evolved well beyond the anabolic window. The evidence now points to a simpler truth: hit your daily target, spread it across the day, and do not skip the overnight feeding. That is where the real gains live.

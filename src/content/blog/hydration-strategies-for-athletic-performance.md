A two percent loss in body mass from dehydration is enough to measurably impair endurance performance, cognitive function, and thermoregulation. By the time an member feels thirsty, they are already behind.

Despite this, hydration remains one of the most poorly managed aspects of sports nutrition. Athletes either drink too little (increasing heat illness risk and performance decrements) or too much (risking exercise-associated hyponatremia). The solution is not "drink more water." It is a structured hydration protocol individualized to the member.

## Assessing Sweat Rate

The foundation of any hydration plan is the member's sweat rate — the volume of fluid lost per hour during exercise. This varies enormously between individuals and is influenced by genetics, fitness level, body size, environmental conditions, and exercise intensity.

To calculate sweat rate:

- **Pre-exercise weight** (nude, after voiding) minus **post-exercise weight** (nude, toweled dry)
- Add the volume of any fluid consumed during exercise
- Divide by the duration of exercise in hours

A typical range is 0.5 to 2.5 liters per hour. Some large members in hot environments can exceed 3 L/hr. Without this data, any hydration recommendation is a guess.

## Electrolyte Considerations

Water alone is insufficient for prolonged exercise. Sweat contains sodium (the primary electrolyte lost), potassium, chloride, and smaller amounts of calcium and magnesium.

Sodium losses vary widely — from 200 mg/L in dilute sweaters to over 1,500 mg/L in salty sweaters. Individuals with visible salt crust on their skin or clothing, a history of cramping, or those who train in heat are likely high sodium losers and need more aggressive replacement.

Practical guidelines:

- **During exercise lasting over 60 minutes:** Include 300-600 mg sodium per liter of fluid consumed
- **For heavy or salty sweaters:** Consider sodium loading before prolonged events (e.g., salty meals the night before)
- **Post-exercise:** Replace 150% of fluid losses over the four to six hours after training. Include sodium to enhance fluid retention

## Pre-Exercise Hydration

Starting exercise euhydrated is more effective than trying to catch up during the session.

- **Two to four hours before:** Drink 5-7 mL/kg body weight (350-490 mL for a 70 kg member)
- **Check urine color:** Pale yellow indicates adequate hydration. Dark yellow or amber signals dehydration.
- **Avoid overdrinking:** Consuming excessive fluid before exercise does not create a reservoir — it creates frequent bathroom trips and potential hyponatremia risk

## During Exercise

The goal during exercise is to prevent body mass loss greater than two percent while avoiding overhydration. A personalized plan based on sweat rate testing is ideal, but general guidelines include:

- **Moderate intensity, cool conditions:** 400-800 mL/hr
- **High intensity, hot conditions:** Up to sweat rate, typically 800-1500 mL/hr
- **Sports drinks (6-8% carbohydrate):** Beneficial for sessions exceeding 60-90 minutes. Provides both fluid and fuel.
- **Small, frequent sips** are better tolerated than large boluses

## The Monitoring Challenge

The most common failure in hydration management is the lack of monitoring. Athletes do not track fluid intake during practice. Sweat rates are never measured. Body weight changes before and after training go unrecorded.
